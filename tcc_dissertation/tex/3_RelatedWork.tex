\label{chap:related}
This chapter reviews the related work in the field of \gls{CRC} detection using \gls{DL} models. It covers the challenges faced by researchers and the various approaches taken to address these difficulties. Furthermore, it presents previous research that used the same \glspl{DS} used for train and validation in this work, as exposed in Chapter \ref{chap:methodology} and \ref{chap:results}. Finally, this chapter highlights the gaps in the existing literature and outlines how this dissertation aims to fill those gaps.

\section{Previous Colorectal Cancer Architectures}
\label{sec_prev_src_arch}

Before \gls{CNN} became dominant approach in \gls{ML} field, handcrafted feature extractor techniques were the standard pattern for image recognition tasks. In 2012 Xu et al. \cite{multipleClusterColon} study approached the \gls{BC} of colon histopathology images, while also identifying revelant tissue regions, via weakly \gls{SL}. Despite the absence of an \gls{ANN} approach, this research is considered one of the earliest \gls{ML} architecture dedicated for automated \gls{CRC} detection.

After the dissemination of the AlexNet architecture in Krizhevsky et al.~\cite{DBLP:conf/nips/KrizhevskySH12}, multiple research were made in \gls{CNN} application fields. Ribeiro et al.~\cite{RibeiroDeepLearning} explored in 2016 the use of \gls{DL} models for robust feature extraction and efficient colonic polyp classification. The main difficulty of the previous feature extraction methods in \gls{CRC} was the proper characterization of these patterns, which could vary from multiple factors as the lack or excess of illumination, the blurring due to movement or water injection, and the appearance of polyps. Even with limit quantity of images samples \gls{DS}, the pretrainned \gls{DL} model outperformed many previous difused cancer classification system and concluded that \glspl{CNN} were a good option for colonic polyp classification.

Korbar et al.~\cite{korbar2017deeplearningclassificationcolorectalpolyps} demonstrated the efficacy of ultra-deep \gls{RNN} for the multi-class classification of colorectal polyps on whole-slide images. Their approach addressed the challenges of processing large-scale histological data, breaking the slides into smaller, overlapping patches. The study concludes that such automated tools can significantly reduce the cognitive burden on pathologists and improve the efficiency of colorectal cancer screening and risk assessment, achieving an accuracy of 93\%, precision of 89.7\%, and an F-score of 88.8\%.

\section{Previous Research on Selected Datasets}
\label{sec_dataset_usage}

Hossain \& Shaban \cite{DBLP:conf/aibthings/HossainS24} compared the results of \glspl{RNN} and the \gls{VGG}16 architecture using the \gls{LC25000} \gls{DS}. Although the primary objective of their article was to find a resource efficient \gls{DL} architecture for \gls{CRC} detection, the results were extremely, exceeding 99\% across all evaluated metrics \cite{metrics}. Given these exceptionally high scores, there is a valid concern regarding whether models trained exclusively on this \gls{DS} can generalize effectively to image sets from alternative sources, a problem that is explored in Chapter~\ref{chap:methodology}.

Kather et al.~\cite{kather2018crc100k} made available a \gls{DS} containing one hundred thousand histological images of human \gls{CRC} and healthy tissue. Researchers Tripathi et al.~\cite{colonML} utilized traditional \gls{ML} algorithms, such as \gls{KNN} and \gls{RFR}, to classify colon tissue, but their study lacks the inclusion of \gls{DL} models. In Chapters \ref{chap:methodology} and \ref{chap:results}, we address this gap by exploring \gls{DL} architectures trained on different available \glspl{DS} and validated with images from external sources~\cite{borkowski2019lungcoloncancerhistopathological, kather_2016_53169}.

Mulyadi \& Utaminingrum~\cite{10913097} explored different configurations of \glspl{RNN} combined with a self-attention layer for colon tissue classification using the \gls{CRC5000} \gls{DS} alongside ImageNet standard normalization \cite{DBLP:conf/nips/KrizhevskySH12}. However, their study did not include classic \gls{CNN} architectures for comparison.

\section{Quanvolution}
\label{sec_quanvolution_related_works}
Sobrinho et al.~\cite{quanvolution} explored the potential of \gls{QML} for breast cancer detection using the BreastMNIST \gls{DS}. Furthermore, the quantum circuit were validated with different resolution images, variating from 28 x 28 pixels to 224 x 224 pixels during train stag. The results suggest that quantum models may offer better generalization capabilities in handling resolution variations, with higher image resolutions not leading to a decline in classification performance.

The research also demonstrated the increasing in train time for higher-resolution images and the capacity of generalization in breast cancer detection, despite the relatively small \gls{DS} used for training.

The conclusion of their work demonstrated that quantum models can achieve competitive performance compared to more complex, traditional \gls{CNN} models. This outcome raises important questions: Can this quantum solution be successfully expanded to other medical use cases, such as \gls{CRC} detection? Furthermore, can it be effectively applied to colon tissue detection?

\section{Gaps explored in this work}
\label{sec_gaps_in_current_literature}
Most current research test the trained model performance with the splitting of the \gls{DS}. However, it lacks on the generalization performance by testing it with different source images. Furthermore, most \gls{DL} studies presents only a single optimizer train in tests, instead of comparing the performance against various.

Moreover, \gls{QML} is an emerging area which lacks contributions on \gls{CRC} classification image. Besides Sobrinho et al.~\cite{quanvolution} in breast cancer detection, \gls{QNN} applicability in colorectal polyps detection still unexplored. Chapters~\ref{chap:methodology} and \ref{chap:results} exploits those gaps by using the same quantum circuit Sobrinho et al.~\cite{quanvolution} with the selected \glspl{DS} presented in this chapter.
